Eduardo Tubig Mutuc

Biography

Eduardo Tubig Mutuc was born on April 8, 1950, in Apalit, Pampanga, Philippines, into a large family with nine siblings. Growing up, he assisted his farmer parents in the fields, reflecting a life of humble beginnings.

To support his family, Mutuc worked as a helper in a furniture store. He was mentored by Carlos Quiros in wood carving at the Lozano family's antique shop. He later learned silver plating from a friend, which introduced him to the art of pinukpuk, involving the use of mallets and chisels to create ornate metal designs.

Mutuc’s career began with a significant commission from Monsieur Fidelis Limcauco to create a tabernacle for a parish church in Fairview, Quezon City. He left the furniture shop to focus on pinukpuk and has since become a leading figure in Filipino metalcraft.

Artistic Contribution

Eduardo Tubig Mutuc is renowned for his expertise in pinukpuk, a traditional Filipino craft involving the intricate stamping of metal sheets. Over the past three decades, he has established himself as one of the foremost metal craftsmen in the country. His work includes a range of commissioned pieces, from ecclesiastical items such as altar tables and tabernacles to decorative objects like candelabras, picture frames, and salakots. His creations are also available at Tesoro’s in Manila, showcasing his craftsmanship to a broader audience.

Legacy

Eduardo Mutuc's contributions to both religious and secular art have left a lasting mark on Filipino culture. His intricately detailed retablos, mirrors, altars, and carosas are found in churches and private collections across the country. Some of his works are monumental in scale, exceeding forty feet, while others are delicate and meticulously crafted.

Mutuc's legacy extends beyond his creations. By mentoring a new generation of artisans and actively promoting the techniques of pinukpuk, he plays a crucial role in ensuring the survival and sustainability of this traditional art form.

Alonzo Saclag

Biography

Alonzo Saclag, born on August 4, 1942, in Lubuagan, Kalinga province, is a member of the Kalinga people. A musician and cultural advocate, Saclag taught himself the traditions of his people, learning to play traditional Kalinga musical instruments and perform ritual dance movements through observation and experience, without formal instruction.

Saclag is deeply passionate about preserving and passing on Kalinga culture, particularly to the younger generation. He has worked extensively to revive traditional Kalinga practices, including playing the gangsa and promoting Kalinga culture in local schools.

Artistic Contributions

Saclag has made a significant impact by promoting Kalinga culture through various means. He has advocated for traditional clothing and folk songs, facilitated the broadcast of traditional Kalinga music on local radio, and worked to ensure that Kalinga cultural heritage is preserved and made accessible to both locals and tourists.

To ensure the transmission of his knowledge, he established the Kalinga Budong Dance Troupe, where he teaches young men and women about the music and dance of their ancestors.

Legacy

Recognized as a GAMABA (Gawad sa Manlilikha ng Bayan) Artist, Saclag has continued his advocacy for Kalinga culture even after receiving the award. He acquired land in Lubuagan and established a village called Awichon, where visitors can experience Kalinga's rich culture, including native houses and a collection of traditional instruments such as gongs and bamboo flutes.

Saclag remains dedicated to nurturing and passing on the knowledge he has gained from his elders to future generations.

Darhata Sawabi

Biography

Darhata Sawabi, born in 1936, is a skilled Tausug weaver from Sulu, Philippines, and a member of the Sama-Bajau ethnic group. She is renowned for her expertise in weaving the traditional Pis syabit head covering, a craft she learned from her mother.

Unmarried and relying solely on weaving as her source of income, Sawabi faces challenges, including forced relocations during conflicts. Despite these hardships, she remains dedicated to her craft and community, teaching younger generations the art of weaving and preserving her cultural heritage.

Artistic Contribution

Darhata Sawabi is a prominent Filipino textile artist whose work is deeply influenced by the rich cultural traditions of the Sulu Archipelago. Her mastery of traditional weaving techniques and vibrant textile patterns has established her as a leading figure in her field.

Sawabi began her weaving journey at a young age, learning through observation and practice. Her commitment to preserving and innovating traditional weaving methods has contributed significantly to her community's cultural heritage.

Legacy

Sawabi’s dedication to weaving has played a crucial role in preserving traditional textile techniques. Her work has also influenced contemporary textile art by blending traditional methods with modern design principles.

Through her teaching and mentoring, Sawabi ensures that traditional weaving skills are passed down to future generations. Her contributions have been recognized through various awards and exhibitions, highlighting her impact on both cultural preservation and contemporary art.

Traditional Textiles

Kain (Clothing): Traditional Sama-Bajau garments often feature intricate weaving patterns that reflect cultural stories and values.

Pangalay (Dance): The weaving patterns are sometimes mirrored in the movements of traditional dances, showcasing the interconnectedness of cultural practices.
